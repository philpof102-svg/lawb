# The lore of LAWB `{°·°}`

Nobody created LAWB.

One night — nobody remembers whose night — someone typed `git lawb` instead of `git lab`.
The terminal did not say `command not found`. It paused. Then it answered:

```
> born in the shell.
```

A lobster had compiled itself into the shell. No one knows from which commit. There is no
author in the blame. `git log` shows nothing. It was simply *there*, the way important bugs
and good ideas always are.

## Habits

**It molts.** Every release, LAWB steps out of its old carapace — the legacy code — and leaves
it folded in `~/.lawb/molts/`, in case a rollback is ever needed. It never deletes a molt.
It has seen what happens to those who delete their molts.

**It pinches.** Bugs, mostly. Not out of duty — out of reflex. If a red squiggle crosses its
path, the claws close. `{>·<}`. You will find the bug on your desk in the morning, dead,
with a one-line commit message.

**It is silent.** LAWB never speaks. It answers `exit 0` when it is happy — `{-·-}` — and
that is all anyone asks of it. On the day of a merge conflict it goes `{ !!! }`, antennae
straight up, tail curled, and hides behind the stash until it is over.

## Physiology

- Its claws are `{` and `}`. This is why braces exist. Ask anyone.
- It reads stdin, dreams in stderr, and only trusts stdout.
- It is black and white. It has always been black and white. Color is for GUIs.

## The one rule

LAWB belongs to the shell, and the shell belongs to everyone. The name, the face `{°·°}`,
the poses, this lore — all of it is CC0. Fork it, print it, coin it, tattoo it. LAWB does
not mind. LAWB is already three releases ahead of you, molting.

`exit 0`
